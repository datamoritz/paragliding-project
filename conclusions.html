This section provides a concise, non-technical synthesis of the project’s results, aimed at pilots and planners.

Relevant Findings

  1. Where was historically thermal lift strongest?

    2. The interactive map shows that thermal lift historically concentrates along the Austrian mountain ridges, especially in well-known flying locations such as Bischling and Zell am See. These hotspots align with common XC routes and ridge structures.

    FIGURE PLACEHOLDER — Spatial thermal clusters map
  2. At what height above ground was thermal lift strongest?

    3. Strong thermals are not restricted to high altitudes. Thermal strength remains remarkably stable from 0–1800 m AGL. Although many thermals begin below 600 m AGL, strong lift (2.6–3.0 m/s) is also observed at 1800 m AGL. This reflects thermals extending vertically through the boundary layer.

    Importantly, new pilots do not need to reach great heights to find strong lift — maximum climb rates occur at all entry heights.

    FIGURE PLACEHOLDER — Climb rate vs entry height
  3. When was lift strongest (time-of-day, seasonality)?

    4. Thermal strength peaks around 11 AM on average, which may reflect both natural heating cycles and pilot behavior (skilled pilots launching during optimal conditions).

    Seasonal patterns are intuitive: spring yields sharper, more aggressive lift due to colder ground and steeper gradients; summer thermals are abundant but softer. Slightly lower summer climb rates may reflect the large number of beginners flying during holidays rather than weaker thermals.

    FIGURE PLACEHOLDER — Seasonality plot
  4. Can clustering reveal thermal types?

    5. Yes — five thermal “types” emerged in the clustering analysis. Full details are available in clustering.html.

    Cluster Characteristics Interpretation
    1 Weak (1.3 m/s), tiny gain, short, high entry Residual/dying lift at altitude.
    2 Strongest (2.6 m/s), moderate gain, short Punchy mid-altitude cores.
    3 Moderate climb, stable, long duration Typical sustained thermals.
    4 Strong, high gain, long duration Classic XC thermals.
    5 Weakest, tiny gain, very short Near-ground broken lift.

  5. How consistent are hotspots over time?

    6. This remains out of scope for this project.

  6. Best takeoff time?

    7. Takeoff times are not recorded in IGC files, so this was out of scope.

  7. Do advanced gliders outperform beginners (EN-A/B)?

    8. The data shows no strong correlation between wing rating and climb performance. Even pilots on EN-A wings demonstrate efficient thermal use and achieve long XC flights. Pilot skill and weather appear to dominate over wing rating in this dataset.

Which Weather Features Predict Strong Thermals?

Two models were trained:

  • Thermal yes/no predictor: shortwave radiation, boundary-layer height, low cloud cover, LCL height, windspeed (10 m)
  • Strong vs weak thermal predictor: dewpoint (2 m), thermal potential ratio, shortwave radiation, LCL height, wind stress proxy (wind²)

Dewpoint and solar radiation consistently rank among the most influential features. Lower dewpoint (drier air) encourages instability and stronger lift, while shortwave radiation drives surface heating that powers convective growth.

Thermal Prediction for Today’s Weather

Yes — we can predict where thermals are likely to form today. The interactive map pulls the current day’s forecast and highlights where strong lift is expected based on historic thermal hotspots.

Predicting exact climb rates was not feasible with this dataset due to sparse atmospheric representation, but this can be improved with larger or higher-resolution weather sources.

Future Work

  • Model wind shear and vertical wind profile using ERA5 pressure levels.
  • Analyze multi-year hotspot stability.
  • Improve climb-rate prediction using denser atmospheric datasets.
  • Integrate terrain shading, lapse rate, and site-orientation data.
  • Expand dataset to other countries and include >1M thermals.
